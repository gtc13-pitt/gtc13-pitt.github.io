CivWiz

Here's the abstract from our group's technical report.

"This technical report describes the redesign and development of CivWiz, a civics learning application originally built as a stand-alone quiz game, into a classroom-ready platform that better supports structured instruction for younger learners. Our central question was how to transform an outdated, socially oriented app into a modern system that teachers can meaningfully use in classrooms without sacrificing the benefits of gamified learning.

At the start of the academic year, CivWiz lacked any classroom infrastructure and relied on a public posting feature that functioned like a lightweight social media feed, raising moderation and safety concerns and distracting from the app’s core purpose. In response, our team clarified requirements with the client, evaluated the existing functionality, and proposed major structural changes that prioritized classroom use over open social interaction.

We implemented these changes using an iterative, client-driven design and development process. First, we created and refined Figma wireframes to define user flows for two primary roles: student and teacher. We then rebuilt the application using Flutter (Dart) with Firebase as the backend. On top of this, we redesigned major interfaces—including landing, play, leaderboard, settings, and classroom pages—to introduce a brighter, more approachable visual style and simplify navigation for younger users.

Finally, we conducted user testing sessions with university students, collecting screen recordings, surveys, and group reflections to identify bugs, usability issues, and desired future features. Our results show that the system now supports core classroom functions: teachers can create classes, approve join requests, assign and review quizzes, monitor performance, and 3 moderate embership; while students can join classes, complete assignments, view progress and missed questions, and see both global and class-specific leaderboards. Regular users are still able to complete quizzes and make progress within the app, but to experience the full breadth of the app, users are recommended to join a classroom.

The app is fully functional in beta and packaged with documentation for continued development, including a catalog of known bugs and user-requested enhancements such as daily challenges, unlockables, and improved admin support.

This work matters because it demonstrates how a narrowly focused, subject-specific learning game can be systematically restructured into a lightweight companion to traditional learning management systems, providing teachers with actionable data and control while preserving the motivational benefits of play for civics education."

Pocket Pantry

Through Google Sites, Google Forms, and Google Sheets, I developed a functioning, medium-fidelity prototype of a reviewing platform and database mobile web app. Although not every individual component is operative, the framework exists, and the intention is apparent. The site can now be used as a template to further develop into a working application.

Food insecurity and uneducated decision-making is apparent within student bodies, so this app’s social intervention and intended argument is to improve upon the pre-existing initiatives that Pitt specifically has in place (the initiatives that benefit the education of the student body on topics such as food insecurity and decisions on where to choose to use meal plan and spend money on campus) and to develop a third-party application that other institutions and university bodies can implement on their campuses.
